CASE STUDY 01

Reducing Accidental Calls on WhatsApp

Accidental voice and video calls are a common disruption while reading chats. This case study explores how a small UX change can prevent unintended actions and improve user confidence.

Role: UX Designer
Platform: Mobile
Tool: Figma
Project Type: Conceptual UX Case Study

PROBLEM

Accidental Calls Happen Too Easily

While scrolling through conversations or reading messages, users often tap the call icon unintentionally. Since calls start immediately, there is no chance to stop the action.

This leads to awkward moments, interrupted focus, and unnecessary user frustration.

UX GAP

No Room for Error

The current call interaction offers no safety net. A low-intent action results in a high-impact outcome.

  • No confirmation step
  • No error recovery
  • Instant call initiation

This breaks a core UX principle: error prevention.

DESIGN GOAL

Prevent Mistakes Without Slowing Users Down

The goal was to design a solution that reduces accidental calls while keeping intentional calls fast and familiar.

  • Reduce unintended calls
  • Preserve speed for intentional actions
  • Feel native to WhatsApp
  • Give users control and confidence
SOLUTION

Confirm Before Calling

A lightweight confirmation dialog appears when the call icon is tapped, asking users to choose between:

  • Voice Call
  • Video Call
  • Cancel

This verifies intent before the call is placed, preventing accidental triggers without adding friction.

IMPACT

More Control, Less Friction

By adding a simple confirmation step:

  • Accidental calls are reduced
  • Users feel safer navigating chats
  • Social discomfort is minimized
  • Overall trust in the interface increases

This demonstrates how small interaction changes can create meaningful improvements in everyday experiences.

CASE STUDY 02

Subscription Discovery: Pressure vs Choice

During the initial launch phase of Swiggy One and Zomato Gold, both platforms introduced subscription benefits that were nearly identical. However, the way these offers were presented resulted in two very different user experiences. This case study compares how early-stage subscription discovery impacted user control, trust, and perception.

Platform: Mobile Apps
Role: UX Designer
Type: Comparative UX Case Study
Method: Personal experience & interface observation

CONTEXT

Subscription Offers During a Core Task

During the early rollout of these subscription programs, a subscription offer appeared immediately after sign-in while attempting to place a food order. Since ordering food is the primary user goal, the timing and presentation of the offer played a critical role in shaping the first impression.

PLATFORM EXPERIENCE

An Interrupted Flow — Swiggy One

Swiggy’s intention behind introducing One was strong, supported by clear value messaging and confident UI-driven marketing during the launch phase. However, the execution surfaced a full-screen subscription offer before users could reach the home screen.

The interface did not provide a clearly visible dismiss option, making it difficult to continue without engaging in the free trial flow.

As a result:

  • The user was prompted to tap “Proceed” to move forward
  • The offer could only be declined later during the payment stage
  • Multiple back actions were required to return to the main app

While the idea and marketing intent were effective, the interaction design introduced friction at a critical moment, creating a sense of pressure rather than choice.

PLATFORM EXPERIENCE

An Optional Invitation — Zomato Gold

During its initial rollout, Zomato introduced Gold as a visible but non-blocking element positioned below the header. Users could continue browsing and ordering without interacting with the subscription offer.

This approach:

  • Kept the core task uninterrupted
  • Clearly communicated optionality
  • Allowed users to engage with the offer at their own pace
UX COMPARISON

Pressure vs Permission

Swiggy One

  • Full-screen interruption
  • Limited early-stage dismissal
  • Guided progression into trial
  • Primary task temporarily blocked

Zomato Gold

  • Inline placement
  • Clearly optional interaction
  • User-led decision making
  • Primary task uninterrupted

These contrasting approaches influenced how users perceived transparency, control, and trust during the early adoption phase of both subscriptions.

UX TAKEAWAYS

Choice Builds Trust

Subscription onboarding during a launch phase is not only about conversion, but about how supported users feel while being introduced to new value. Strong ideas and marketing, when paired with respectful interaction design, can drive both adoption and long-term trust.

This comparison highlights how subtle UX decisions during early rollouts can significantly shape user perception, even when the underlying offer remains the same.
